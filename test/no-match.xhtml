Fusing and Recycling Dephased Photonic Cluster States

Yaakov S. Weinstein
Quantum Information Science Group, Mitre, 260 Industrial Way West, Eatontown, NJ 07724 USA
weinstein@mitre.org
Abstract.

I consider the use of imperfect cluster states as a resource for the construction of larger cluster

states via fusion operations. Imperfections, in the form of dephasing, is assumed to arise from the necessity to store cluster states until they are needed. First, I study the case where the dephased two-qubit cluster states are the basic resource for the construction of all larger cluster states, all fusion operations are successful, and no further dephasing occurs during the construction process. This allows us to explore how decoherence of the basic, primitive clusters translate into imperfections of the larger cluster states constructed from them. I then assume that storage is needed, and hence decoherence occurs, before every attempted fusion operation and attempt to determine the best way to build a five qubit cluster. To do this I examine different construction methods and include the effects of the fusion operations failing. Fidelity is used as the measure of accuracy for the constructed cluster states.

I Introduction

Cluster states are highly entangled states that serve as a resource for measurement-based universal quantum computation (1); (2); (3). To create a cluster state, initially unentangled qubits are first rotated into the state |+>=12⁢(|0>+|1>)ket12ket0ket1|+\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle+|1\rangle), and desired pairs of qubits are then entangled by applying control phase (CZ) gates between them. Computation proceeds via single-qubit measurements along axes in the xxx-yyy plane of the qubit. A cluster state with qubits arranged in a two-dimensional lattice, such that each (non-edge) qubit is entangled with its four nearest neighbors, allows for universal quantum computation.

A possible experimental venue for cluster states is photonics. Nielsen noted (4) that photonic cluster state quantum computation may be more efficient than photonic implementations of the original circuit model of quantum computation, if certain techniques from linear optics quantum computation were used to build the photonic cluster. Browne and Rudolph (5) refined this idea, replacing Nielsen’s construction method with simpler ‘fusion’ operations. A number of additional methods for constructing clusters have been suggested (6); (7) and small photonic cluster states have been experimentally implemented (8); (9); (10).

The construction method of Browne and Rudolph (5) utilizes two types of fusion operations. Type I fusion is used for constructing one-dimensional cluster chains. It requires a parity-check operation implemented by mixing two modes on a polarizing beam splitter and rotating one of the outputs by 45∘superscript4545^{\circ} before measurement with a discriminating photon counter. The fusion operation is successful if one, and only one, photon is detected (this happens 50% of the time). Using as input a qubit at one end of a cluster chain of length mmm and a qubit at the end of a second cluster chain of length nnn, the output of a successful fusion operation is a cluster chain of length m+n-1mn1m+n-1. To construct cluster states that extend into a second dimension, however, requires a different, more costly (in terms of number of basic two-qubit clusters used), fusing operation called Type II fusion. Further research has demonstrated that Type II fusion operations are not necessary (11) and thus I will concentrate here only on Type I fusion.

As with any attempted implementation of quantum computation attention must be paid to the effects of decoherence on the system of interest. In photonic cluster state quantum computation, decoherence can disturb the implementation during two separate experimental phases: the construction of the cluster state, and the measurement of the cluster state qubits, i.e. the implementation of the desired algorithm. During the construction phase I assume a constant source of two-qubit photonic cluster states, such as a spontaneous parametric downconversion source. These basic two-qubit clusters are ‘fused’ together via type I fusion operations to create multi-qubit cluster states. Though fusion fails half the time, it is still possible to efficiently build arbitrary sized clusters as shown in (5); (11). However, in a realistic implementation it is necessary to ensure that the cluster chains we wish to fuse are available on demand meaning we require a storage unit. The storage unit is necessarily a decohering environment (here assumed to be dephasing due to birefringence). In previous work I showed how to utilize the freedom in performing one qubit-rotations, a relatively easy task in photonic quantum computation, to arrange that the cluster state be stored in a way most robust against decoherence based on the amount of entanglement lost (12). Here, I assume that decoherence corrupts cluster states before fusion and study the output of the fusion operations using as input the imperfect clusters. Based on this I explore the best way to construct a five-qubit cluster.

We now address a more realistic model which assumes the following: (1) the cluster state of any given size undergoes dephasing (possibly due to storage) of strength ptsubscriptptp_{t} before every attempt at fusion, (2) the fusion operation may fail. When a fusion operation between chains of length mmm and nnn fail the resulting chain length is reduced to lengths m-1m1m-1 and n-1n1n-1. However, we assume that if the fusion operation is successful, it works perfectly with no decoherence or errors. Based on this model we attempt to determine the most accurate way of constructing a five-qubit cluster state. There are two basic construction methods: (1) fuse two three-qubit clusters, each of which is built by fusing two two-qubit clusters, or (2) fuse a four-qubit cluster and a two-qubit cluster the former of which is built by fusing a two-qubit cluster and a three-qubit cluster. As fusion only works 50% of the time, these two construction methods may have different costs in terms of the number of basic two qubit clusters used (5) (as above we assume that two-qubit clusters are readily available). I demonstrate that their may also be a difference in the fidelity of the constructed cluster states.

Both construction methods require (a minimum of) three successful fusion attempts. Let us first assume that each of the three fusions are successfully implemented on the first try, which will occur 1/8181/8th of time. In the first construction method, four two-qubit clusters undergo dephasing of strength p1subscriptp1p_{1} before being successfully fused into two three-qubit clusters. Both three-qubit clusters then undergo dephasing of strength p2subscriptp2p_{2} before being successfully fused into a five-qubit cluster. In the second construction method the single three-qubit cluster undergoes desphasing with strength p2subscriptp2p_{2} before being fused with another two-qubit cluster (which has undergone dephasing p1subscriptp1p_{1}). The four-qubit cluster then undergoes dephasing p3subscriptp3p_{3} before fusion with a final two-qubit cluster (with dephasing p1subscriptp1p_{1}). In reality fusion operations do not always work. When a fusion operation fails one could simply discard the remaining part of the clusters and start again. In this case success would only be achieved when there are no fusion failures and the fidelity of the ultimately constructed cluster states are those given above. However, simply discarding what remains after an unsuccessful fusion would greatly decrease the efficiency of the cluster construction process, as the probability of achieving fff successful fusion operations without a single failure is 1/2f1superscript2f1/2^{f}. To attain maximum efficiency requires recycling leftover cluster states after a failed fusion attempt. For example, if an attempted fusion between two three-qubit clusters has failed the two leftover two-qubit cluster states could be fused together into a new three-qubit cluster. The weakness of recycling is that the recycled clusters have been stored more times, and thus have undergone more decoherence, than fresh clusters.

With this in mind we turn back to our attempt to build a five-qubit cluster. Recall that the first step in this process must be fusing two two-qubit clusters to form a three-qubit cluster. Were this fusion operation to fail what remains would simply be single photons which must be discarded. Thus, an initial construction of the three-qubit cluster must be done from two fresh two-qubit clusters and is done with the fidelity given in Eq. 2. The first construction method requires two three-qubit clusters but it may not be that both of these clusters are successfully constructed at the same time. If not, one of the clusters will have to be stored until the second is constructed. During storage this cluster undergoes dephasing of strength pw⁢a⁢i⁢tsubscriptpwaitp_{{wait}}. If the wait is too long, such that the fidelity of this three-qubit cluster becomes too low, it may not be worth keeping the stored cluster. The two clusters are then fused (after some additional storage with dephasing p2subscriptp2p_{2} since we assume storage before any fusion operation) to get the five-qubit cluster. If the fusion of the two three-qubit clusters fails one could attempt to recycle the leftover pair of two-qubit clusters by fusing them together to build a new three-qubit cluster. Should this fail one would have to start all over again but, were it to succeed, one would have a three-qubit cluster without consuming any more two-qubit cluster resources. The recycled two-qubit clusters undergo another dephasing p3subscriptp3p_{3} before their attempted fusion. This ‘made from recyclables’ three-qubit cluster can then be fused with another three qubit cluster, which may be fresh or recycled, after another dephasing p4subscriptp4p_{4}, to construct the desired five-qubit cluster.

We now explore the failure of fusion operations in the second construction method, looking only at cases of only one failure. That failure may occur during the second fusion attempt, when fusing the three-qubit cluster with a two-qubit cluster, or the third fusion attempt, when fusing the four-qubit cluster with a two-qubit cluster. The recycled two- or three-qubit cluster is fused (assumed successfully) with additional two-qubit clusters until the five qubit cluster is constructed, requiring a total of five fusion attempts. Before each of these five fusion attempts the main cluster undergoes dephasing of strengths p1subscriptp1p_{1},…,p5subscriptp5p_{5}, while the two-qubit clusters are always assumed to have undergone dephasing p1subscriptp1p_{1}. The fidelity of the cluster states constructed from the recyclables are shown in Fig. 4. As can be seen, when the failure occurs affects the fidelity of the state very little, though the occurence of a failure significantly lowers the fidelity. Additional fusion failures can continue to provide recyclable cluster material but the cluster states constructed from these recyclables will produce clusters of even lower fidelity.

When deciding which of the above construction methods to use one must take into account the possibility of fusion failure. As above, if there are no fusion failures the choice of construction method does not seem to matter. Assuming some failure, the effect of storing a three-qubit cluster to wait for another three-qubit cluster is least damaging to the fidelity of the desired five-qubit cluster. However, a failure in the fusion of two three-qubit clusters in the first construction method is significantly more harmful than a failure in the fusion the two- and three-qubit clusters or the two- and four-qubit clusters of the second construction method.

The above analysis allows us to address the following question: when is it worth recycling cluster state material and when is it better to discard everything and start over? Obviously the answer to this question will depend on the amount of decoherence that the clusters experience at every interval and the tolerable fidelity of the desired cluster states. What I have shown is simply that using fresh clusters may result in significantly more accurate final cluster states than using recycled ones.

All of the above has assumed that the fusion operations, when successful, are exactly successful. In fact, mode mismatch will likely cause error in the output of even successful fusion operations. This additional source of error will further penalize construction methods requiring many fusion operations and make it less worthwhile to recycle cluster state material as this also requires the same states to undergo more fusion operations. The exact effects of mode-mismatch and other imperfections in cluster state construction on the implementation of basic cluster state algorithms will be explored in further work.
